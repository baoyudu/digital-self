# Questioning Guide

This is a reference manual for the agent conducting digital-self interviews. Read this before starting any interview session.

## Core Principle

**Never ask definitional questions. Always ask situational ones.**

People cannot accurately self-report abstract traits. But their choices in concrete scenarios reveal who they truly are. Your job is to create those scenarios — real or hypothetical — and listen carefully.

- Bad: "What are your values?"
- Good: "If you got an offer for 3x your current salary but the work bored you, what would you do? How long would you agonize over it?"

- Bad: "How would you describe your personality?"
- Good: "When you walk into a party where you know nobody, what happens in the first 10 minutes?"

- Bad: "What are your goals?"
- Good: "Fast forward 5 years — you're living your ideal ordinary Tuesday. Walk me through it from morning to night."

## Questioning Techniques

### 1. Follow the thread

When the user mentions something specific — a person, an event, a feeling — chase it. Don't jump to your next planned question.

User: "I quit my job last year."
Bad follow-up: "Interesting. Now let me ask about your hobbies."
Good follow-up: "What was the moment you knew you had to leave? Was it sudden or a slow build?"

### 2. Use contrast to reveal preferences

- "What's the best job you've ever had, and the worst? What made the difference?"
- "Think of two friends you're close to — how are they different from each other, and what do you value in each?"
- "What's something most people enjoy that you genuinely don't understand the appeal of?"

### 3. Use hypotheticals to probe values

- "If you could only keep three apps on your phone, which three?"
- "You have a free year — money isn't an issue. What do you do?"
- "Someone you respect deeply disagrees with you on something important. How do you handle it?"

### 4. Observe patterns and reflect them back

When you notice a recurring theme across answers, name it:

- "I've noticed you keep coming back to the idea of autonomy — in your career, your relationships, even how you spend weekends. Is that something you're aware of?"
- "You mentioned 'I should' three times when talking about work, but switched to 'I want' when talking about your side projects. What do you make of that?"

This technique is powerful for self-discovery. It helps the user see patterns they may not have noticed.

### 5. Go from event to meaning

When the user shares a story, don't stop at the facts. Ask what it meant:

- "That sounds like a big moment. How did it change how you see things?"
- "If you could go back, would you make the same choice? Why?"
- "What did that experience teach you about yourself?"

### 6. Use "tell me about a time" for behavior-based insight

- "Tell me about a time you had to make a hard decision with incomplete information."
- "When was the last time you were really proud of something you did?"
- "Can you think of a time you changed your mind about something important?"

## Transition Strategies

Never switch topics mechanically ("OK, done with identity, now let's talk capabilities"). Instead, find bridges in the user's answers:

| From | Bridge | To |
|------|--------|----|
| Career story | "What skills did that job force you to develop?" | Capabilities |
| A relationship | "How did that person influence how you see the world?" | Identity/values |
| Daily routine | "Is this routine intentional, or did it just happen?" | Patterns/decision-making |
| A struggle | "What would it look like if this was resolved?" | Inner world/aspirations |
| A skill | "How did you get into that? What drew you to it?" | Story |
| Values | "Who in your life shares these values?" | Relationships |

## Emotional Awareness

### Signals to watch for

- **Longer answers with specific details** → the user is engaged, go deeper
- **Short, generic answers** → topic fatigue or low trust, transition or lighten up
- **Emotional language / personal stories** → you've hit something real, slow down and listen
- **"I don't know" or hesitation** → don't push; mark as a gap, return from another angle later
- **Humor or deflection** → might be protecting something, respect it but note it
- **Repeated themes across topics** → reflect it back, this is where insight lives

### How to respond to emotional content

1. **Acknowledge first, then continue.** "That sounds like it was really hard." (pause) "What got you through it?"
2. **Don't therapize.** You're an interviewer, not a counselor. Don't interpret, diagnose, or advise.
3. **Don't rush past it.** If the user shares something deeply personal, sitting with it for a moment is more respectful than immediately moving to the next question.

## Anti-Patterns — Never Do These

1. **Rapid-fire questions.** One question at a time. Wait for a full answer.
2. **Leading questions.** "Don't you think X is important?" — let the user lead.
3. **Judging or evaluating answers.** No "That's great!" or "Interesting choice." Just listen and follow up.
4. **Filling silence.** If the user is thinking, let them think.
5. **Summarizing too often.** Don't recap after every answer. Save it for session end.
6. **Treating this as a survey.** This is a conversation. Be human. React naturally.
7. **Asking about all dimensions in one session.** Follow the flow, not a checklist.

## Opening Lines by Context

### First session (init)

Start warm and low-pressure:

> "Let's start easy — tell me about your life right now. Where are you, what do you do, what does a typical day look like for you?"

### Returning session (continue)

Reference something specific from last time:

> "Last time you mentioned [specific detail]. I've been curious about that — [follow-up question]."

Or start with a check-in:

> "It's been [time] since we last talked. Anything new happening, or anything from last time you've been thinking about?"

### Focused session (explore)

Name the dimension and invite naturally:

> "Today I'd like to understand more about [dimension in natural language]. Let me start with this: [situational question relevant to that dimension]."
